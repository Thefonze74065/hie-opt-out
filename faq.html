Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions and answers about HIE opt-out procedures

You should contact all healthcare providers who might have your medical information, including:

Primary Healthcare Providers:

  • Primary Care Physicians - Family doctors, internists, pediatricians
  • Specialists - Cardiologists, endocrinologists, dermatologists, etc.
  • Mental Health Providers - Psychiatrists, psychologists, therapists, counselors
  • Emergency and Urgent Care - Hospital systems, urgent care centers

Specialty Care Providers:

  • Dental Care - Dentists, oral surgeons, orthodontists
  • Eye Care - Optometrists, ophthalmologists
  • Physical Therapy - Physical therapists, occupational therapists
  • Alternative Medicine - Chiropractors, acupuncturists (if they use EHR systems)
  • Women's Health - OB/GYNs, reproductive health clinics

What to Ask Each Provider:

  1. Do you participate in any Health Information Exchange networks?
  2. Do you share my information through Epic Care Everywhere, CareQuality, or other HIE systems?
  3. How can I opt out of any health information sharing?
  4. Can you provide written confirmation of my opt-out preference?

Pro Tip: Many smaller providers (especially dental and vision) may not participate in major HIE networks, but it's worth asking. The largest risk is usually from major hospital systems and large healthcare networks.

Medicare beneficiaries face multiple layers of health data sharing, some of which are harder to avoid than others:

Medicare Claims Data (Hard to Avoid):

  • Medicare Parts A & B: Claims data is automatically collected by CMS (Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services)
  • Medicare Advantage (Part C): Private insurers also collect comprehensive data
  • Medicare Part D: Prescription drug data is tracked
  • This data is used for billing, fraud prevention, and healthcare research

What You CAN Control:

  • Provider HIE Sharing: Opt out of Epic Care Everywhere, CareQuality, and state HIEs
  • Medicare Shared Savings Program: Ask if your providers participate and opt out if possible
  • Quality Reporting Programs: Some have opt-out provisions
  • Research Databases: Opt out of additional data sharing for research

Federal Programs to Ask About:

  • Blue Button 2.0: Medicare's API program (you control this)
  • Chronic Care Management: Some programs share data with care coordinators
  • Medicare Quality Programs: Hospital quality reporting and value-based care

Action Steps for Medicare Beneficiaries:

  1. Contact each of your healthcare providers to opt out of HIE sharing
  2. Ask your Medicare Advantage plan (if applicable) about data sharing policies
  3. Review any care management programs you're enrolled in
  4. Consider supplemental insurance privacy policies
  5. Be aware that basic Medicare claims data will still be collected

Important: You cannot completely avoid federal health records if you use Medicare benefits. The goal is to minimize additional sharing beyond what's required for billing and basic program administration.

Legally, no. Healthcare providers cannot deny you care for opting out of HIE sharing. However, there are practical considerations:

What Won't Change:

  • Quality of medical care you receive
  • Access to treatments, procedures, or medications
  • Your provider's ability to access their own records of your care
  • Emergency care (providers can still treat you)

What Might Change:

  • You may need to bring medical records to new appointments
  • Providers might need to repeat some tests if they can't access previous results
  • You'll need to be more proactive in sharing relevant medical history
  • Coordination between specialists might require more effort on your part

Best Practices After Opting Out:

  • Keep your own copy of important medical records
  • Maintain a list of current medications, allergies, and diagnoses
  • Request records from previous providers when seeing new ones
  • Consider a personal health record app or folder

Here's how to verify your opt-out was successful:

Request Written Confirmation:

  • Ask for written confirmation from each provider's Privacy Officer
  • The confirmation should specify which HIE networks you've opted out of
  • Keep copies of all opt-out forms and confirmations

Follow Up Timeline:

  • Week 1-2: Submit opt-out forms
  • Week 3-4: Follow up if no acknowledgment received
  • Month 2: Request written confirmation of processing
  • Month 6: Verify status hasn't changed with annual check-ins

Test Your Opt-Out Status:

  • When visiting a new provider, ask if they can see your records from other systems
  • Request to see what information appears in their system about you
  • Check patient portals for any external record sharing

This is a crucial distinction that many people find confusing:

HIPAA (Federal Law):

  • Allows sharing for "treatment, payment, and healthcare operations" without your consent
  • Covers how your existing provider handles your information
  • Requires you to be notified about privacy practices
  • Does NOT require consent for most HIE sharing

HIE Opt-Out (State Laws):

  • Restricts sharing beyond the immediate provider relationship
  • Covers electronic sharing with other healthcare systems
  • Requires providers to honor your opt-out preference
  • Goes beyond HIPAA's baseline protections

Key Takeaway:

HIPAA actually permits most HIE sharing, while state HIE opt-out laws give you the right to prevent that sharing. You need HIE opt-out procedures, not just HIPAA requests.

Yes! HIE opt-out decisions are generally reversible, though the process varies by provider and state:

How to Opt Back In:

  • Contact the same Privacy Officer who handled your opt-out
  • Request to reverse your HIE opt-out preference
  • Fill out any required forms (may be the same form with different selection)
  • Verify the change with written confirmation

What to Expect:

  • Processing time similar to original opt-out (1-4 weeks)
  • Your information will start being shared again going forward
  • Previous medical records may or may not be retroactively shared (varies by system)
  • You can opt out again in the future if desired

Partial Opt-In Options:

Some systems may allow you to:

  • Opt in to sharing with specific providers only
  • Share only certain types of information (emergency info only)
  • Set time-limited sharing preferences

This is surprisingly common, especially with front desk staff. Here's how to handle it:

Escalate to the Right Person:

  • Ask specifically for the "Privacy Officer" or "HIPAA Privacy Officer"
  • Request the "Health Information Management" department
  • Ask to speak with the Practice Manager or Office Manager
  • Don't accept "we don't do that" from front desk staff

Use Specific Terms:

  • "Epic Care Everywhere" (if they use Epic EHR)
  • "CareQuality network participation"
  • "Electronic health information exchange"
  • "Interoperability with other healthcare systems"

Reference Your Rights:

  • Mention your state's specific HIE opt-out law (if applicable)
  • Ask them to check with their legal or compliance team
  • Request written confirmation that they don't participate in any HIE networks

Red Flags (They Probably DO Participate):

  • They use Epic, Cerner, or other major EHR systems
  • They're part of a large hospital system
  • They mention being able to see your records from other providers
  • They ask if you want them to "look up" your information
