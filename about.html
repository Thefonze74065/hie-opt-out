About Health Information Exchange

Understanding how your medical information is shared and your rights to control it

What HIE Does

Health Information Exchanges allow healthcare providers, hospitals, and other medical professionals to electronically share your medical records, diagnoses, medications, and visit history across different healthcare systems.

Your Privacy Rights

Most states allow you to opt out of HIE sharing while still receiving quality healthcare. Providers cannot deny you care for choosing to protect your privacy.

Why Consider Opting Out?

Protect sensitive information from unnecessary sharing. Your dentist doesn't need to know about reproductive health visits, and your podiatrist doesn't need STI/STD history.

How HIE Works

Electronic Health Records (EHR)

Most healthcare providers use electronic health record systems like Epic, Cerner, or Allscripts. These systems can share information with other providers using the same or compatible systems.

  • Epic Care Everywhere - connects Epic systems nationwide
  • CareQuality - interoperability framework
  • CommonWell - health data sharing alliance
  • State HIE networks - government-run exchanges

What Information Is Shared

HIE networks typically share comprehensive medical information including:

  • Visit summaries and diagnoses
  • Prescription medications
  • Lab results and imaging
  • Immunization records
  • Allergies and medical history
  • Mental health records (in some cases)

Legal Framework

HIPAA Protections

The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) provides baseline privacy protections, but allows information sharing for "treatment, payment, and healthcare operations" without explicit patient consent.

Key point: HIPAA allows most HIE sharing by default, which is why state laws are important for opt-out rights.

State Laws

Many states have enacted additional privacy protections that give patients the right to opt out of HIE sharing:

  • Illinois - 740 ILCS 110/9.6 (effective Aug 2023)
  • North Carolina - NC Health Information Exchange Act
  • California - CMIA opt-out protections
  • Many other states have similar laws

Benefits vs. Privacy Concerns

Potential Benefits of HIE

  • Faster access to medical history in emergencies
  • Reduced duplicate testing and procedures
  • Better coordination between specialists
  • More complete picture for healthcare providers
  • Potential for better population health insights

Privacy and Security Concerns

  • Increased risk of data breaches
  • Potential misuse of sensitive information
  • Sharing beyond immediate healthcare needs
  • Discrimination based on health history
  • Lack of granular control over what's shared
  • Unknown future uses of collected data

Making an Informed Decision

Consider Your Personal Situation

The decision to opt out of HIE sharing is personal and depends on your individual circumstances:

You might want to opt out if:

  • You have sensitive health conditions you want to keep private
  • You prefer to manually share records when necessary
  • You're concerned about data security and breaches
  • You want maximum control over your health information
  • You have experienced discrimination based on health status

You might want to stay opted in if:

  • You frequently see multiple specialists or providers
  • You have complex medical conditions requiring coordination
  • You travel frequently and might need emergency care
  • You're comfortable with the current level of sharing
  • You find the convenience benefits outweigh privacy concerns

Remember: You can always change your decision. Many states allow you to opt back in to HIE sharing if you later decide the benefits outweigh your privacy concerns.
