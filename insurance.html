Health Insurance Privacy

Control how your insurance company shares your health data and claims information

Insurance companies collect comprehensive health data through claims processing and often share this information with third parties for "care coordination," research, and business purposes - sometimes without your explicit consent.

What Insurance Companies Share

  • Medical Claims: Every doctor visit, test, procedure, and treatment you've received
  • Prescription Claims: All medications covered by insurance, including mental health and reproductive health drugs
  • Diagnostic Codes: ICD-10 codes revealing your medical conditions and diagnoses
  • Provider Networks: Which doctors and hospitals you visit most frequently
  • Healthcare Utilization: How often you seek medical care and for what conditions
  • Cost Data: What you and your insurance pay for various treatments
  • Demographic Information: Age, gender, location, and family composition

Who Gets Your Insurance Data

  • Healthcare Providers: Doctors can access your claims history for "care coordination"
  • Pharmacy Benefit Managers (PBMs): Companies like CVS Caremark, Express Scripts, OptumRx
  • Data Analytics Companies: Firms that analyze health trends and create patient profiles
  • Research Organizations: Universities and pharmaceutical companies for studies
  • Government Agencies: Medicare, Medicaid, and public health departments
  • Other Insurance Companies: Through Medical Information Bureau (MIB) and similar databases
  • Employers: Group health plan sponsors may receive aggregate data

How to Limit Insurance Data Sharing

1

Review Your Insurance Privacy Notice

Every insurance company must provide a Notice of Privacy Practices. Read it carefully to understand how your data is shared.

What to look for: Sections on "Uses and Disclosures" and "Your Rights" - these explain data sharing and opt-out options.
2

Contact the Privacy Office

Call or email your insurance company's privacy office (not general customer service) to discuss data sharing limitations.

What to say: "I want to opt out of all data sharing for research, marketing, and quality improvement. I also want to limit care coordination data sharing to only my explicitly authorized providers."
3

Request Written Authorization Requirements

Ask that your insurance require written authorization before sharing your data with any third parties.

Key point: This means they must ask your permission each time before sharing data, rather than having blanket consent.
4

Opt Out of Research and Quality Programs

Specifically request to opt out of research studies, quality improvement programs, and population health initiatives.

Important: These programs often share de-identified data, but de-identification isn't always perfect.
5

Review and Update Annually

Insurance companies change their privacy policies regularly. Review your preferences annually and after any policy changes.

Reminder: Set a calendar reminder to review your insurance privacy settings each year during open enrollment.

What You Can and Cannot Control

‚úÖ What You CAN Control

  • Data sharing for research and quality improvement programs
  • Marketing and advertising uses of your health data
  • Sharing with business associates for non-treatment purposes
  • Participation in health and wellness programs
  • Some aspects of care coordination data sharing
  • Release of information to family members

‚ùå What You CANNOT Control

  • Claims processing and payment operations
  • Fraud detection and prevention activities
  • Government reporting requirements (Medicare, Medicaid)
  • Public health reporting (infectious diseases, etc.)
  • Legal proceedings and court orders
  • Emergency treatment situations
  • Insurance underwriting for future coverage

  • Review Employer Plan Options: Some employer plans offer better privacy protections than others
  • Consider Health Savings Accounts (HSAs): HSA payments may have different privacy implications
  • Understand Medicare/Medicaid Implications: Government programs have different privacy rules
